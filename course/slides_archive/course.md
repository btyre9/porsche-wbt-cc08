## Slide 04 — The FFB Framework
Slide-ID: SLD_CC08_004
Template-ID: card-explore
Slide-Title: The Feature-Function-Benefit Framework
Caption-Text: The Feature-Function-Benefit framework gives every explanation a clear structure.
On-Screen-Text: Every technical explanation you give should answer three questions. Click each card to learn what they are.
Image-File: ffb_framework_cards_CC08.webp
Notes: card-explore chosen — three parallel, equally weighted concepts that each require individual exploration. Learner must click all three before advancing. Interaction keeps engagement without requiring video assets.
Status: Draft
Voiceover-INTRO: The Feature-Function-Benefit framework is your foundation for every technical explanation. It gives you a structure that moves the customer from "what is it" to "why it matters to me." Click each card to explore the three parts.
Voiceover-CLICK-Feature: The Feature is the component, part, or service itself — what you actually found or what you're recommending. For example: "Your rear shock absorbers are leaking fluid." This is where you start — state clearly what needs attention.
Voiceover-CLICK-Function: The Function explains what that component does — technically or mechanically. Why does it exist? What happens when it works correctly? For example: "Shock absorbers dampen suspension movement and keep your tires in consistent contact with the road, especially in corners."
Voiceover-CLICK-Benefit: The Benefit — or Consequence — connects the feature and function to what the customer actually cares about. This is the most important part. Customers don't buy features; they buy what features do for them. "When these leak, you lose handling precision and risk uneven tire wear. For a performance car like yours, that directly affects the driving experience you bought the car for."
Card-Title-Feature: What You Found
Card-Sig-Feature: Feature
Card-Bullets-Feature: The component, part, or service you're recommending | State it clearly and specifically — no jargon | Example: "Your rear shock absorbers are leaking fluid."
Card-Title-Function: How It Works
Card-Sig-Function: Function
Card-Bullets-Function: What the part does mechanically or technically | Why does it exist in the vehicle system? | Example: "Shock absorbers keep your tires in consistent contact with the road."
Card-Title-Benefit: Why It Matters
Card-Sig-Benefit: Benefit
Card-Bullets-Benefit: Connects the feature to what the customer actually cares about | Customers buy outcomes, not components | Example: "This directly affects the driving experience you paid for."

## Slide 08 — Using Analogies
Slide-ID: SLD_CC08_008
Template-ID: content-split
Slide-Title: Make the Technical Tangible
Caption-Text: Analogies connect the unfamiliar to the familiar.
Image: A side-by-side visual pairing a technical component with a familiar everyday object — for example, a worn tire next to worn athletic shoe soles, or a clogged air filter next to a dirty cloth mask.
Notes: content-split with Pull-Quote chosen — the pull-quote reinforces the key takeaway without restating VO. The slide introduces the concept of analogies before the applied scenario slides that follow.
Status: Draft
Voiceover-INTRO: When a customer looks confused or uncertain, an analogy can be the fastest path to understanding. Analogies work because they connect something unfamiliar — a technical system — to something the customer already knows from their own life. An air filter works like a cloth over your mouth in dusty air. Brake fluid absorbs moisture like a sponge, which lowers its boiling point and reduces stopping power. Tires with worn tread are like athletic shoes that have lost their grip. You don't need a library of analogies — just a few well-chosen ones that you're comfortable using naturally.
Pull-Quote: A good analogy doesn't simplify the problem — it makes it real.
